October 12, 1963 – August 24, 2010

Perfect Blue is a 1997 Japanese animated psychological horror film directed by Satoshi Kon and written by Sadayuki Murai.

The story is about a girl named Mima who left her music group “CHAM!” to start her career as an actress. She plays a rape victim role on TV and her fans become incredibly upset and angry. With the pressure of her new career, her reputation on the line and a potential stalker, Mima’s psychological state is put into question.

Perfect Blue completely went against the grain of other anime films. It focused on real-life subjects that reflected issues within Japan’s pop-idol images and online culture. For Kon, Perfect Blue was not focused on the idea of the celebrity but more about a person’s public self and real self, and in Mima’s case, the gaps between these identities become non-existent. Kon experimented with match cuts and intercutting to make the storytelling all the more convincing. Perfect Blue is by far Kon’s most disturbing film.

Millennium Actress is a 2001 Japanese animated film co-written and directed by Satoshi Kon and produced by Madhouse.

It tells the story of two documentary filmmakers investigating the life of a retired acting legend named Chiyoko Fujiwara based on the beloved Japanese actress Setsuko Hara. As Chiyoko tells them the story of her life, the difference between reality and cinema becomes blurred.

Distinct from his other films, Millennium Actress is known for pushing the idea of Kon’s seamless editing style, using an optical illusion technique called trompe-l’oeil motif that translates as ‘deceive the eye’. Different from the blurring of realities that confuse Mima in Perfect Blue, the editing here is used to show Chiyoko’s control of her identity as an actress and her real self as she effortlessly travels between them.

Chiyoko’s character is known as Kon’s running woman, as she is constantly and literally running from one scene into the next. Combined with Kon’s fluid editing style, her running seems to break time and space.

Tokyo Godfathers is a 2003 Japanese anime film directed by Satoshi Kon loosely based on Peter B. Kyne’s novel Three Godfathers.

On the night of Christmas Eve, Gin, Hanna and Miyuki (a hopeless drunk, a trans-woman and a teenage runaway) find a baby in a trash pile and sets off a journey to find the baby’s mother.

Tokyo Godfathers has a linear narrative carried by the three main characters, each having an own identity that is fundamental to the story and themes of the film, making them the most human and relatable characters out of all of Kon’s films.

Unique to Tokyo Godfathers is Kon’s character Hanna, a trans-homeless woman, who is not only one of the funniest characters in Kon’s films but is treated with immense complexity. Her desired gender is not only used as a comedic trope, but her traumatic past and trans-identity are vital to the film’s themes of family and motherhood.

Paprika is a 2006 Japanese science-fiction anime film co-written and directed by Satoshi Kon. It is Kon’s fourth and final feature film before his death in 2010.

In Paprkia, a new invention (DC mini) enables psychologists and scientists to view people’s dreams. A thief uses DC minis to enter people’s minds illegally. A scientist named Chiba attempts to catch the thief and retrieve the stolen tech along with the help of a hyperactive dream girl named Paprika.

Kon makes the compassion between reality and dreams. Using the idea of dreams as a suppressed consciousness, the dreams of Paprika are colorful, fantastical and wild. The main heroine, Paprika, is a duality of the scientist Chiba, but instead of battling the counterpart, both women work together as a pair. Chiba is a reserved and analytical scientist, with her counterpart Paprika being the complete opposite; outgoing and untamed. Paprika breezes through scenes, free of the laws of time and space.

In Memory of Satoshi Kon
information

Satoshi Kon was a Japanese film director, animator, screenwriter and manga artist from Sapporo, Hokkaido and a member of the Japanese Animation Creators Association (JAniCA). Kon’s dramatic anime films offered biting social commentary, fantastical dreamscapes, and glimpses of his vision that people live in multiple realities.

Over the course of Kon’s life he made four films, all examining dualities: identities on-screen and off screen in the shocking and provocative Perfect Blue, public and private life in the quietly beautiful Millennium Actress, reality and fantasy in comedy Tokyo Godfathers, and the virtual and the real in the hyperactive Paprika.

In May 2010, Satoshi Kon was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer and given half a year to live. At the time he was working on a new film titled Dreaming Machine; however, he did not have much time left. Kon composed a final message to his fans that was to uploaded to his blog upon his death.

He died on August 24, 2010, at the age of 46.
